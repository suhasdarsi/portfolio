If you're into backpacks, travel gear, or everyday carry (EDC), you've probably heard of brands like Aer, Peak Design, Bellroy, Matador, Alpaka, or Evergoods. They make fantastic products, but they're also premium brands with premium prices.

During my trip to Ho Chi Minh City, I came across a Vietnamese brand called Yuzi, and after spending a few hours at their warehouse, I walked away genuinely impressed.

This isn't a sponsored review. I bought everything with my own money, spent several hours talking to one of their employees about their products and design philosophy, briefly met the CEO before leaving, and have now been using the products for around ten days.

## First Impressions

The first thing you'll notice is that Yuzi's products feel familiar.

Their design language reminds me a lot of Aer—clean, minimal, and functional. The bags are a little more squared-off compared to Aer's softer curves, but the inspiration is obvious.

Some people may see that as criticism.

Personally, I don't.

Almost every country that develops a strong consumer brand ecosystem goes through this phase. Companies begin by learning from the best products in the world. Over time, they refine those ideas, adapt them to local customers, develop their own identity, and eventually build products that stand on their own.

Yuzi feels like it's somewhere along that journey.

---

## "We're Trying to Build Global Brand Quality for Vietnamese People"

I spent most of my visit talking with one of the employees, who walked me through almost every product they make.

At one point I asked what the company was ultimately trying to achieve.

His response really stuck with me.

> "We're trying to build global brand quality for Vietnamese people."

That sentence perfectly explained what I was seeing.

Rather than simply making the cheapest possible bags, they seemed focused on delivering products that could stand beside international brands while remaining accessible to customers in Vietnam.

---

## Customer Feedback Drives Their Products

One conversation stood out.

I was looking at one of their travel backpacks that reminded me of the Aer Travel Pack 3 and noticed it used YKK AquaGuard zippers.

Personally, I'm not a huge fan of AquaGuard zippers. That's purely a personal preference—I tend to prefer traditional coil zippers because I worry about the coating eventually wearing or peeling after years of heavy use.

So I asked why they chose AquaGuard.

The answer had nothing to do with aesthetics.

They explained that one of the most common requests from customers was better protection against rain.

Vietnam experiences heavy rainfall throughout much of the year, so customers wanted bags that handled wet weather better.

The decision wasn't made because AquaGuard looks premium.

It was made because customers kept asking for it.

Whether you agree with the decision or not, I appreciated that it came directly from customer feedback.

---

## Constantly Improving Their Products

Another thing I really enjoyed was seeing multiple generations of products.

Instead of pretending every design was perfect from day one, they openly showed me Version 1 and Version 2 of several products.

They explained:

- What customers complained about
- What materials had been upgraded
- What construction methods had changed
- Why certain features were added
- What lessons they learned from earlier versions

As someone who enjoys product design, I found that fascinating.

Good hardware companies iterate.

Yuzi clearly has that mindset.

---

## Better Materials Than I Expected

While walking around the warehouse, I noticed rolls of X-Pac fabric and asked about sourcing costs.

The numbers they quoted were roughly what I'd expect internationally, which gave me confidence they weren't quietly substituting cheaper materials.

Across everything I handled, the quality was impressive.

I noticed:

- Genuine YKK hardware
- Good-quality fabrics
- Clean stitching
- Well-finished construction
- Good attention to detail

I've now used my purchases for about ten days, and so far they've left a very positive first impression.

Long-term durability is something only time will answer, but the initial quality certainly exceeded my expectations.

## Visiting the Warehouse

If you're planning to visit Yuzi yourself, there are a few things worth knowing.

First, this isn't a polished retail showroom.

It's their warehouse, tucked away inside a small alley well outside Ho Chi Minh City's main tourist areas.

Whether you take Grab or public transport, expect a fairly long journey. It's not somewhere you'll casually stumble across while sightseeing.

Another thing to keep in mind is that they didn't accept credit or debit cards when I visited.

If you're planning to buy products in person, carry enough cash.

Honestly, unless you're interested in seeing the products before buying or talking with the team like I did, I'd recommend ordering through their official Shopee store instead.

Prices are generally a little lower there, and delivery within Ho Chi Minh City is usually very quick.

## Final Thoughts

After spending a few hours with the team, briefly meeting the CEO before leaving, and using the products for around ten days, I came away impressed.

Yuzi still feels like a company that's evolving. The design language clearly takes inspiration from some of the best travel gear brands in the world, but what impressed me more was their willingness to listen to customers and continuously improve their products.

That product mindset is much harder to copy than the shape of a backpack.

I still want to compare some of these products directly with equivalents from Aer and Matador over a much longer period before making any claims about long-term durability.

But as a first impression, Yuzi exceeded my expectations.

If you're travelling through Vietnam and enjoy travel gear, backpacks, or EDC, I think they're well worth checking out.

And if you don't feel like making the trip out to their warehouse, just order through Shopee—you'll probably save yourself both time and money.
